Before we start

Your name and the team you lead

01

Where the work struggles

G1What goes wrong, or goes slower than it should, in your team's work? Name the two or three problems that cost you the most time or money.

The problems you name here are what the training plan is built to fix, so this is your team's claim on the budget.

G2For each problem above: would better skill or knowledge actually fix it? If the fix is really equipment, staffing or a process, say so plainly.

Saying "this is not a training problem" saves the budget for what training can fix, and sends the real issue to the right place instead of burying it under a course.

G3Where the fix is skill: who on your team needs what, exactly?

Names and specifics mean the right person gets the right training, instead of everyone sitting through something most did not need.

02

What has been tried, and who can teach

G4What training did your team get in the last year, and what problem did it solve?

Shows what has already been tried, so the plan does not repeat what did not work.

G5Who on your team is good enough at something that they could teach it to the others?

Your own strong people are the cheapest and most convincing trainers available; naming them puts them on the teaching roster.

03

Cover, and requests worth hearing

G6If you were away for a month, what would not get done, who comes closest to being able to do it, and what would that person still need to learn to cover it properly?

The honest answer protects your team from depending on one head, including yours; it feeds the succession and continuity plan the owner keeps, and tells us what cross-training the stand-in still needs.

G7Anything a member of your team has asked to learn that you think deserves a hearing?

Requests that come with a manager's backing get looked at first.
